  • Starters

    • Caldo Verde

      Soup with collard greens, cabbage, onions, potatoes, and chouriço.

      Caldo Verde rivals bacalhau as the national dish of Portugal. Although it is originally from the Minho province in northern Portugal, caldo verde is also a very popular soup in Lisbon.

    • Cataplana

      Stew of clams, chouriço, ham, onion, garlic, and tomato, with parsley and chili pepper.

      The cataplana seafood dish is a popular, traditional meal from the Algarve coast. Cataplana recipes vary from one region to another. For example, in the Western Algarve, locals often add potatoes and/or shrimp and mussels. This dish is not to be confused with the name of the special cookware used to prepare it!

    • Caipirinha

      Cocktail made with sugar, lime, and cachaça.

      Although originally imported from Brazil, this cocktail has become a big hit in Portugal. There are several variations on the caipirinha in Lisbon, including the "caipirão" (with licor Beirão), the "caipiroska" (with vodka), the "caipinegra" (with blackberry vodka), and the "morangoska" (with vodka and fresh strawberries).

  • Fish

    • Bacalhau à Brás

      Shredded salted cod, rice, scrambled eggs, and onions, with a black olive garnish.

      Bacalhau, or "codfish", is the basis of Portugal's economy, and the most popular fish in Portuguese cuisine. In fact, it is often said that the Portuguese have 1001 ways to serve cod - much more than enough for each day of the year.

    • Pastéis de Bacalhau

      Pastries of cod, potato, egg with parsley, served with rice.

      These delicious codfish pastries are often served as a starter, but they are also a fantastic main course when served with rice. One of the reasonscodfish became so popular in Portugal and other Roman Catholic countries was because of the many days when eating meat is forbidden by the Church.

    • Caldeirada de Bacalhau

      Stew of cod and potatoes.

      Like many other Portuguese dishes, the specific fish used in caldeirada may vary from one town to the next depending on what fish is available. The original recipe for caldeirada was considered "the rich man's stew," but it has since become a popular dish for everyone.

    • Chocos com Tinta

      Grilled chocos with potatoes and salad.

      Grilled cuttlefish is a delicious alternative to bacalhau. Although not always served this way, here the cephalopod is grilled over charcoal and served in a sauce made from its own ink. Beware, because the inky sauce can dye your teeth!
  • Meat

    • Bitoque

      Steak with egg on top, served with french fries, rice and salad.

      No Lisboan menu would be complete without this egg-topped steak. This popular dish might be one of the most commonly served dishes in Portugal, and makes a great meal any time of day.

    • Arroz de Pato

      Rice with duck, ham, and chouriço, with parsley and a mint garnish.

      This traditional meal originated in Braga, a region in northwestern Portugal. Although called "duck rice," this dish is usually made with a combination of different meats, and is generally accompanied by a variety of vegetables, and is seasoned with various herbs and spices.

    • Feijoada

      Stew of beef, pork, and beans.

      The word "feijão" is Portuguese for "beans" - so it is not surprising that beans feature heavily in this dish. Like many Portuguese meals, there are a variety of ways to make this traditional food. For example, in northwest Portugal, feijoada is usually made with white beans, whereas in the northeast, it is generally prepared with kidney beans and other vegetables, such as tomatoes, carrots, and cabbage.

    • Cozido à Portuguesa

      Stew of beef, chicken, pork, chouriço, collard greens, potatoes and carrots.

      This dish, which is also very popular in Spain, originated in the Beira region. It consists mainly of pork and beef shins, and may be accompanied by rice, potatoes, and a variety of vegetables. However, the dish ingredients vary from one region to another.
  • Wine

    • Vinho Alvarinho

      Crisp, refreshing green wine from Monção.

      Green wine is made from grapes that do not contain large amounts of sugar, and therefore do not require the aging process. This is one of the most popular green wines in Portugal.

    • Vinho Monte Velho

      Red wine from Alentejo.

      Portugal has been a major wine exporter since the time of the Roman Empire. There are a great many wine varieties in the country.

    • Sangria

      Wine with sugar, apple, orange, lemon and orange soda.

      Sangria is an excellent alternative to more alcoholic options. Although it is a very popular in locations around the world, each place gives the concoction a unique twist. Making sangria with apple, orange, and lemon is a more typical method of preparing this refreshing drink here in Lisbon.
  • Dessert

    • Arroz Doce

      Rice pudding flavored with lemon zest and cinnamon.

      This traditional Portuguese favorite came from Asia and the Middle East during the Crusades. Older variations were made with molasses, and therefore had a much darker color. Now, it is usually made with white sugar. Arroz doce is particularly popular in Portugal during Christmas.

    • Leite-creme

      Egg custard topped with hard caramel.

      Leite-creme is the Portuguese version of the French crème brûlée. It is one of the most popular desserts in Portugual, along with other rich egg-based desserts, such as pudim flã, arroz doce, and the famous pastel de nata.

    • Pastel de Nata

      Egg and cream pastry with cinnamon.

      Pastéis de nata were probably first invented before the 18th century by Catholic monks at the Jerónimos Monastery. These pastries were mass produced during medieval times by convents and monasteries with large amounts of leftover egg yolks. The famous Casa Pastéis de Belém began to take over the manufacture of the pastries as more and more convents and monasteries closed in the 1800s.
